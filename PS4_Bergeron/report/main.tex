\section{The Huggett Model}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Setup}

We solve the infinite-horizon Huggett (1993) model. Each household maximises
discounted expected utility
\begin{equation}
    \max_{\{a_{t+1}\}_{t=0}^{\infty}} \; \mathbb{E}_0 \sum_{t=0}^{\infty} \beta^t u(c_t)
    \label{eq:hh_problem}
\end{equation}
subject to the period budget constraint
\begin{equation}
    c_t + a_{t+1} = (1+r)a_t + \exp(z_t), \quad c_t \geq 0, \quad a_{t+1} \geq a_{\min},
    \label{eq:budget}
\end{equation}
where $z_t$ is idiosyncratic log-income following the AR(1) process
\begin{equation}
    \log z_t = \rho \log z_{t-1} + \varepsilon_t, \quad \varepsilon_t \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\sigma^2).
    \label{eq:ar1}
\end{equation}

The utility function is CRRA with risk-aversion parameter $\gamma$:
\begin{equation}
    u(c) = \frac{c^{1-\gamma}}{1-\gamma}.
    \label{eq:utility}
\end{equation}

Household optimality is characterised by the Euler equation
\begin{equation}
    u'(c_t) = \beta(1+r)\,\mathbb{E}_t\bigl[u'(c_{t+1})\bigr],
    \label{eq:euler}
\end{equation}
which holds with equality whenever the borrowing constraint $a_{t+1} \geq a_{\min}$
does not bind.

Bonds are in zero net supply, so the goods market clears when
\begin{equation}
    \sum_{i} \sum_{j} a_i \,\phi(a_i, z_j) = 0,
    \label{eq:huggett_mc}
\end{equation}
where $\phi(a,z)$ is the stationary joint distribution of assets and income.
The equilibrium interest rate $r^*$ is defined as the unique $r$ satisfying \eqref{eq:huggett_mc}.

\subsection{Calibration}

The model is calibrated at annual frequency. Parameters are set as follows:

The borrowing limit is set to average income, $a_{\min} = -\bar{z} \approx -1.30$,
reflecting that households cannot borrow more than one year's mean income.
The AR(1) process is discretised using Rouwenhorst's method with $n_z = 7$ grid points.
The asset grid uses a polynomial spacing with curvature $\kappa = 2$, which concentrates
points near the borrowing constraint where the policy function is most curved.

\subsection{Algorithm}

\paragraph{Value function iteration.}
We solve the household's problem using value function iteration (VFI) with Howard
policy improvement. Starting from an initial guess $V^0(a_i, z_j) = u(Ra_i + \exp(z_j) - a_{\min})/(1-\beta)$,
each outer iteration performs a full Bellman maximisation exploiting the monotonicity
and concavity of $V$ in $a$, followed by $M = 30$ Howard improvement steps under
the fixed policy. This reduces the iteration count by roughly an order of magnitude
relative to standard VFI.

\paragraph{Equilibrium.}
We find $r^*$ using bisection on the interval $[r_{\text{low}}, r_{\text{high}}] = [-0.05,\; 1/\beta - 1 - \epsilon]$.
At each candidate $r$, we solve the household problem, compute the stationary distribution
via the eigenvalue method, and evaluate net asset demand $A(r) = \sum_{i,j} a_i \phi(a_i, z_j)$.
Bisection terminates when $|r_{\text{high}} - r_{\text{low}}| < 10^{-4}$.

\paragraph{Stationary distribution.}
Given the converged policy function $g(a,z)$, we construct the state-transition matrix
$P$ using linear interpolation weights between adjacent grid points, and compute the
stationary distribution $\phi$ as the unit eigenvector of $P'$. A warning is issued
if the unit eigenvalue is not unique.

\subsection{Results}

\paragraph{Equilibrium interest rate.}
The equilibrium interest rate is $r^* \approx -0.044$. The negative interest rate
reflects the strength of the precautionary savings motive: households accumulate
precautionary wealth even at negative real returns, so the bond market clears below
the risk-free rate that would prevail under complete markets. The gap between $r^*$
and $1/\beta - 1 \approx 0.042$ measures the precautionary savings wedge.

\paragraph{Value function.}
Figure \ref{fig:v_fxn} shows the value function across wealth levels for each income
state. The value function is increasing and concave in $a$, as expected from CRRA
utility and diminishing marginal utility of wealth. Higher income states shift the
value function upward, since wealthier households in better income states can afford
higher consumption in all future periods.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.75\linewidth]{../programs/figs/Value_Function_graph.png}
    \caption{Value function $V(a,z)$ for each income state $z_j$, plotted against
    current asset holdings $a$. The function is increasing and concave in $a$, with
    higher income states corresponding to uniformly higher values.}
    \label{fig:v_fxn}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Policy function.}
Figure \ref{fig:policy_fxn} shows the savings policy function $g(a,z)$ for the
lowest and highest income states. Several features are notable. First, the policy
function lies below the 45-degree line for low asset levels, meaning constrained
households dissave to finance consumption. Second, the policy function crosses the
45-degree line at an interior point: households with assets above this threshold
accumulate further wealth. Third, higher income states shift the policy function
upward, reflecting that high-income households save more at every wealth level.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.75\linewidth]{../programs/figs/Policy_fxn_graph.png}
    \caption{Savings policy function $g(a,z)$ for low and high income states.
    The dashed 45-degree line marks the locus $a' = a$; the policy function lies
    below this line where households dissave and above it where they accumulate.}
    \label{fig:policy_fxn}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Stationary distribution.}
Figure \ref{fig:phi_dist} shows the stationary distribution $\phi(a,z)$.
The distribution displays the right-skewed shape typical of incomplete-market models:
a large mass of households near the borrowing constraint and a long thin tail of wealthy
households. The mass at the constraint reflects households in persistent low-income
states who cannot smooth consumption fully.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.75\linewidth]{../programs/figs/Phi_Dist_graph.png}
    \caption{Stationary distribution $\phi(a,z)$ for low and high income states.
    The distribution is concentrated near the borrowing limit, with a thin right tail
    of wealthy households.}
    \label{fig:phi_dist}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Wealth grid binding.}
The lower end of the wealth grid (the borrowing limit $a_{\min} = -\bar{z} \approx -1.30$)
is binding for a non-trivial fraction of households, confirming that the precautionary
savings motive is not strong enough to keep all agents away from the constraint.
The upper end of the wealth grid is not binding: the policy function never reaches
$a_{\max}$, confirming the grid is wide enough. This can be verified by checking
that the policy function lies strictly below $a_{\max}$ for all $(a_i, z_j)$ and
that the stationary distribution has negligible mass near $a_{\max}$.

\section{The Aiyagari Model}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Setup}

The Aiyagari (1994) model extends the Huggett framework to a production economy.
A unit measure of households face the same problem as in \eqref{eq:hh_problem}--\eqref{eq:ar1},
but now $z_t$ represents idiosyncratic log-labour supply rather than earnings,
and factor prices are determined endogenously by a representative firm.

The household budget constraint becomes
\begin{equation}
    c_t + a_{t+1} = (1+r)a_t + w\exp(z_t), \quad c_t \geq 0, \quad a_{t+1} \geq 0,
    \label{eq:aiyagari_budget}
\end{equation}
where $r$ is the return to capital and $w$ is the equilibrium wage, both taken
as given by the household. The borrowing constraint is set to zero, $a_{\min} = 0$.

A representative firm with production function
\begin{equation}
    F(K,L) = K^\alpha L^{1-\alpha}
    \label{eq:production}
\end{equation}
hires capital and labour in competitive markets. Profit maximisation implies
\begin{equation}
    r = \alpha K^{\alpha-1} L^{1-\alpha} - \delta, \qquad
    w = (1-\alpha) K^\alpha L^{-\alpha}.
    \label{eq:firm_focs}
\end{equation}

Aggregate labour supply is $L = \mathbb{E}[\exp(z)]$ under the stationary distribution
of the income process. Inverting the first equation in \eqref{eq:firm_focs} gives
the capital demand function
\begin{equation}
    K(r) = \left(\frac{\alpha L^{1-\alpha}}{r + \delta}\right)^{\frac{1}{1-\alpha}}.
    \label{eq:capital_demand}
\end{equation}

Equilibrium requires that the capital market clears:
\begin{equation}
    K^* = \sum_{i} \sum_{j} a_i \,\phi(a_i, z_j) = \mathcal{A}(r^*, w^*),
    \label{eq:aiyagari_mc}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{A}(r,w)$ is aggregate household asset demand at prices $(r,w)$.

\subsection{Calibration}

The Aiyagari model uses the same household parameters as the Huggett model
($\gamma$, $\beta$, $\rho$, $\sigma$, $n_z$, $n_a$, $\kappa$), with the
following additions and modifications:

\subsection{Algorithm}

\paragraph{Equilibrium computation.}
We find $K^*$ using bisection. At each candidate capital stock $K$, we compute
factor prices $(r, w)$ from \eqref{eq:firm_focs}, solve the household problem,
and evaluate aggregate asset demand $\mathcal{A}(r,w)$.
The excess demand function is
\begin{equation}
    \text{ED}(K) = \mathcal{A}(r(K), w(K)) - K,
    \label{eq:excess_demand}
\end{equation}
which is decreasing in $K$ (higher capital lowers $r$, reducing the return to
saving, so households demand fewer assets). We bisect until $|K_{\text{high}} - K_{\text{low}}| < 10^{-4}$.

\paragraph{Labour income scaling.}
Since $f\_spe$ treats $\exp(z)$ as labour income, we pass $\tilde{z} = z + \log w$
so that $\exp(\tilde{z}) = w\exp(z)$, the correct labour income. This avoids
modifying the solver's internal structure.

\paragraph{Wealth distribution.}
The stationary distribution is computed identically to the Huggett case
via the eigenvalue method. Inequality statistics are computed from the
marginal distribution $\phi(a) = \sum_j \phi(a, z_j)$.

by a precautionary savings wedge. Relative to the complete-markets steady state,
the Aiyagari economy features a higher capital stock because the precautionary
motive drives additional saving.

\paragraph{Wealth inequality statistics.}

Table \ref{tab:inequality} reports the Gini coefficient and top wealth shares.
The Gini coefficient is computed from the Lorenz curve using the trapezoidal rule:
\begin{equation}
    \text{Gini} = 1 - 2\int_0^1 L(F)\,\mathrm{d}F,
    \label{eq:gini}
\end{equation}
where $L(F)$ is the Lorenz curve (share of wealth held by the bottom $F$ fraction
of the population).

The top 10\% and top 1\% wealth shares are computed as
\begin{equation}
    \text{Top-}p\% \text{ share} = 1 - L(1 - p/100).
    \label{eq:top_share}
\end{equation}

Standard Aiyagari models with CRRA utility and an AR(1) income process typically
generate Gini coefficients around 0.35--0.45, substantially below the US empirical
value of approximately 0.78. This is a well-known limitation of the model. The
discrepancy arises because the model abstracts from several empirically relevant
features, including entrepreneurial risk (Quadrini, 2000), life-cycle effects with
heterogeneous earnings profiles (Huggett, 1996; De Nardi, 2004), luxury bequest
motives (De Nardi, 2004), and private returns to capital (Benhabib et al., 2019).
These extensions can substantially improve the model's fit to the upper tail of the
wealth distribution.

\paragraph{Wealth grid binding.}
The upper bound of the wealth grid ($a_{\max} = 60$) is not binding at equilibrium:
the policy function never reaches $a_{\max}$ and the stationary distribution has
negligible mass near the upper bound. This confirms that the grid is wide enough
and that the chosen $a_{\max}$ does not distort equilibrium allocations.
The lower bound $a_{\min} = 0$ is binding for low-income households, as expected.
